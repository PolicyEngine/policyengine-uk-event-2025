Welcome and vision

Max Ghenis, Co-founder & CEO, PolicyEngine

Max Ghenis presents PolicyEngine's journey from its origins at the UBI Centre in 2020 to becoming an independent nonprofit in 2021. The presentation traces key milestones: early policy scoring demonstrations in the UK, expansion to the US in March 2022, building rigourous economic simulation with representative datasets, and launching multiple access surfaces including the Python package and AI summarisation in 2023. Ghenis highlights growing adoption by organisations including the Green Party, Centre for Policy Studies, NIESR, IEA, Social Market Foundation, Liberal Democrats, and UK in a Changing Europe, culminating in HM Treasury piloting PolicyEngine UK in March 2025. He announces the Nuffield Foundation's grant enabling expansion with childcare integration, public services modelling, local datasets, validation dashboards, and AI-enhanced design.

Technology and AI for UK policymaking: The PolicyEngine approach

Max Ghenis, Co-founder & CEO, PolicyEngine
Nikhil Woodruff, Co-founder & CTO, PolicyEngine

Nikhil Woodruff demonstrates how PolicyEngine uses artificial intelligence and modern software architecture for UK policy analysis. Building on OpenFisca-Core, PolicyEngine processes policy simulations in real-time. Woodruff shows the platform's AI-powered explanation system, launched in 2024, which translates tax and benefit calculations into clear language. He explains how PolicyEngine uses machine learning for data enhancement, addressing the Family Resources Survey's undersampling of high-income households, while maintaining efficiency across millions of policy configurations.

Scope of the model: Policies, households, and dynamics

Vahid Ahmadi, Research Associate, PolicyEngine

Vahid Ahmadi presents PolicyEngine UK's modelling scope, covering the platform's tax and benefit rules from income tax and National Insurance to Universal Credit, Council Tax support, and childcare programs. Ahmadi explains how PolicyEngine maintains current policy while enabling simulation of hypothetical reforms through its OpenFisca-based architecture.

Ahmadi details PolicyEngine's data enhancement capabilities, addressing under-sampling and measurement errors through machine learning. Using random forest imputation and gradient descent reweighting, PolicyEngine fuses the Family Resources Survey with the Wealth and Assets Survey and Living Costs and Food Survey. He explains behavioural response modelling for labour supply and capital gains tax, noting how PolicyEngine's Capital Gains Tax analysis showed behavioural modelling projected lower five-year revenue than static analysis.

Localising policy impact: Parliamentary constituencies and local authorities

Nikhil Woodruff, Co-founder & CTO, PolicyEngine
Ben Ogorek, Data Scientist, PolicyEngine

Nikhil Woodruff and Ben Ogorek present PolicyEngine's local area modelling framework, estimating tax and benefit policy impacts at the parliamentary constituency and local authority level. The system reweights Family Resources Survey data to match administrative statistics from HMRC, ONS, ASHE, and the House of Commons Library, using gradient-based optimisation across 11,000 targets covering income, age, and employment patterns.

Woodruff demonstrates the interactive dashboard for exploring local area data and comparing impacts across regions. Applications include enabling MPs to understand constituency-specific effects and helping local authorities assess benefit changes.

Ogorek presents how PolicyEngine US is building upon this infrastructure by cloning households for each local area rather than reweighting. This approach reveals programme participation patterns at the congressional district level, such as food stamp (SNAP) benefits by district. The underlying software powering this capability is being made available for usage beyond PolicyEngine. PolicyEngine plans to incorporate this approach into UK data in 2026, enabling more granular local area analysis.

Biniam Gebre, Co-Founder, Citizen Codex

NIESR's use of PolicyEngine in its living standards review

Max Mosley, Senior Economist, New Economics Foundation

Max Mosley, Senior Economist at the New Economics Foundation and formerly at NIESR, presents how PolicyEngine supported NIESR's UK Living Standards Review 2025. The review found the poorest households in Slovenia and Malta are now better off than the poorest in the UK, real income growth has been among the slowest in Europe, and the UK ranks as neither a high wage nor high welfare country.

Mosley demonstrates how PolicyEngine enabled NIESR to analyse policy interventions, particularly poverty reduction measures. Analysis showed removing the two-child limit would be the most cost-effective poverty reduction approach. He discusses how PolicyEngine's accessibility improved the efficiency of NIESR's analysis and communication of findings.

Microsimulation of a carbon dividend

Malcolm Torry, Co-founder and Director Emeritus, Citizen\

Malcolm Torry, formerly at the Basic Income Earth Network and London School of Economics, presents PolicyEngine's application to carbon tax and dividend proposals, where carbon tax revenue funds unconditional payments. Torry analyses two scenarios: a £100 per tonne tax funding £18.60 per week dividends, and a £200 per tonne tax funding £37.40 per week. While both scenarios redistribute from rich to poor, some low-income households would experience losses. Torry demonstrates how microsimulation informs policy design, showing precisely which households gain and lose under different approaches.

VAT analysis with synthetic firm microdata

Vahid Ahmadi, Research Associate, PolicyEngine

Vahid Ahmadi presents PolicyEngine's firm-level microsimulation for VAT policy, analysing threshold changes between £70,000 and £120,000 for 2026-27. Using synthetic firm microdata calibrated to ONS and HMRC statistics, the model captures firms by industry, turnover, employees, and VAT liability. PolicyEngine's estimates deviate an average of 12.3% from HMRC projections. Raising the threshold to £100,000 would remove 48,000 firms from VAT registration and reduce revenue by £371 million, while a £70,000 threshold would add 109,000 firms and generate £642 million.

PolicyEngine US: Tools for deconstructing complex policies

David Trimmer, Policy Research Fellow, PolicyEngine
Daphne Hansell, Research Analyst, PolicyEngine

David Trimmer and Daphne Hansell demonstrate PolicyEngine US's household-level analysis tools through two recent applications.

Trimmer presents the household-by-household interactive for the One Big Beautiful Bill Act (OBBBA), which allows users to explore the legislation's effects on over 40,000 representative households using PolicyEngine's Enhanced Current Population Survey data. The interactive's "Get Random Household" function reveals how the same policy affects different families across America's diverse population, with each household weighted by population representation.

Hansell demonstrates a tool for simulating the extension of enhanced premium tax credits, which expire after 2025 and are a central subject in the US's current government shutdown. The tool shows how different extension scenarios affect health insurance subsidy impacts at the individual household level. These tools make policy impacts tangible by showing real effects on specific family types rather than aggregate statistics alone.

Looking ahead: AI-powered policy analysis

Max Ghenis, Co-founder & CEO, PolicyEngine
Nikhil Woodruff, Co-founder & CTO, PolicyEngine

Max Ghenis and Nikhil Woodruff present PolicyEngine's AI roadmap across three pillars: building software, improving models, and augmenting software.

For building software, they announce the beta launch of the policyengine-claude plugin at github.com/policyengine/policyengine-claude, providing custom commands, specialised agents, and domain knowledge for PolicyEngine development. They demonstrate a scientific approach to rules-as-code automation: creating golden pull requests with manually verified code, using AI to generate implementations from policy descriptions, then iteratively adjusting prompts to maximise alignment between AI-produced and golden code.

For improving models, they present two priorities: continuing US local area modelling work with enhanced calibration methods while transferring these to UK constituencies and local authorities, and exploring synthetic panel generation to enable lifetime program simulation (such as US Social Security solvency) where current cross-sectional survey data provides only snapshots.

For augmenting software, they preview PolicyEngine App v2's natural language capabilities: LLM extraction to pull relevant insights from the database based on natural language queries, and flexible visualisations to present insights through adaptive charts on an extensible report canvas. The presentation concludes with reflections on AI's promise—enabling unprecedented policy analysis that is more comprehensive, accurate, and accessible—balanced against challenges as AI transforms society through changing income patterns and creating new demands for policy research aligned with societal values.
