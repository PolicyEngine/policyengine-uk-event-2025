Welcome and vision

Speaker: Max Ghenis

PolicyEngine is a non-profit providing free, open-source microsimulation tools that make tax and benefit policy analysis accessible to policymakers, researchers, journalists, and citizens. PolicyEngine UK is the UK's only open-source tax-benefit microsimulation model, built on the OpenFisca framework, combining a web interface with an engine processing millions of policy reforms in real-time. The platform uses the Family Resources Survey enhanced through fusion with other datasets and machine learning techniques. The Nuffield Foundation's December 2024 grant of £251,296 has enabled enhancements to the UK model, supporting local authority and constituency datasets, integration of childcare subsidies and public services, an open validation dashboard, and AI-capable design improvements.

PolicyEngine's impact has grown: recognition from HM Treasury, analysis of policy moments, and expansion to cover all 50 US states with tax-benefit modelling. The organisation has expanded into environmental policy with carbon tax modelling, firm-level microsimulation for VAT analysis, and behavioural response modelling. PolicyEngine 2.0, with AI-powered explanations, enhanced user experience, and expanded analytical capabilities, represents a step toward making evidence-based policy analysis part of democratic discourse.

Technology and AI for UK policymaking

Speaker: Nikhil Woodruff

PolicyEngine leverages artificial intelligence and software architecture to transform UK policy analysis. Building on the OpenFisca-Core framework, the platform employs a technical stack that processes policy simulations in real-time, enabling users to receive instant feedback on reform proposals. The AI-powered explanation system, launched in 2024, translates tax and benefit calculations into language, making analysis accessible regardless of technical background.

Modelling the UK's tax and benefit system presents challenges due to interactions between means-tested benefits, tax credits, and universal programs. PolicyEngine employs machine learning for data enhancement, including addressing the Family Resources Survey's known undersampling of high-income households. The platform's Python-based architecture maintains computational efficiency while supporting millions of policy configurations. Technical decisions enable PolicyEngine to serve both individual users seeking personal calculations and researchers conducting population-wide microsimulation analysis.

Scope of the model: Rules, data, and dynamics

Speaker: Vahid Ahmadi

PolicyEngine UK's modelling scope encompasses a range of tax and benefit rules encoded in the platform. The platform captures the complexity of the UK fiscal system, from income tax and National Insurance to Universal Credit, Council Tax support, childcare programs (Tax-Free Childcare, Extended childcare entitlement, Universal childcare entitlement, Targeted childcare entitlement, and Care to Learn), and numerous other means-tested and universal programs. The methodology maintains an up-to-date representation of current policy while preserving the flexibility to simulate any hypothetical reform through the platform's modular architecture built on OpenFisca.

PolicyEngine's data enhancement capabilities represent one of its features, addressing challenges in microsimulation: under-sampling of demographic groups and measurement errors including underreporting of income and benefits. Using machine learning techniques (specifically random forest algorithms for imputation and gradient descent optimisation for reweighting), PolicyEngine enhances the Family Resources Survey by fusing it with the Wealth and Assets Survey and Living Costs and Food Survey, correcting underrepresentation of high-income households and reducing deviations from official statistics. The platform's behavioural response modelling incorporates elasticities for income effects on labour supply, substitution effects on labour supply, and capital gains tax responses, with all parameters customisable by users. PolicyEngine's recent Capital Gains Tax analysis showed behavioural modelling projected lower five-year revenue compared to static analysis, illustrating the importance of capturing how individuals adjust economic decisions when facing new incentives.

Live demo: PolicyEngine v2 platform

Speaker: Anthony Volk

PolicyEngine 2.0 features a redesigned platform based on user research conducted by partner organisation Citizen Codex. The new design addresses user feedback requesting an interface with lower experience and domain knowledge thresholds. The user journey from designing a policy reform to interpreting its economic and distributional impacts demonstrates how the new interface reduces the time and understanding needed for users to run their first microsimulation.

Improvements to the platform's design and functionality include visualisation tools, navigation reflecting web standards, and new features designed to lower barriers for first-time users. Capabilities include customising household circumstances to understand policy impacts on specific family types, comparing multiple policy scenarios side-by-side with clarity, and using the API to integrate PolicyEngine analysis into external research workflows. New features include sign-in functionality for saving and sharing policy scenarios, mobile optimisation for analysis on any device, and increased opportunities for AI integration into the user workflow, demonstrating PolicyEngine's commitment to democratising access to policy analysis tools while maintaining the technical rigour that researchers require.

UX research and design of PolicyEngine v2 platform

Speakers: Biniam Gebre & MaSara Myers

User experience research informed the design of PolicyEngine 2.0, revealing insights from interviews with policymakers, researchers, journalists, and citizens to understand their needs, pain points, and mental models when engaging with policy analysis tools. These insights shaped design decisions, from information architecture to visual design, ensuring that microsimulation results are presented in ways that work for users.

The balance between simplicity and sophistication in policy analysis interfaces enables PolicyEngine 2.0 to serve both casual users seeking quick policy insights and expert researchers requiring detailed technical outputs. Specific design patterns were developed to make distributional analysis, budget impacts, and household-level effects intuitive, with ongoing efforts to improve the platform based on user feedback.

Localising policy impact

Speaker: Nikhil Woodruff

PolicyEngine's local area modelling framework estimates the impact of UK tax and benefit policies at the level of parliamentary constituencies and local authorities, transforming how policymakers understand the geographical distribution of policy effects. The system reweights national Family Resources Survey data to match administrative statistics on income, employment, and population for each local area, creating household weights that maintain consistency with national totals while reflecting local characteristics. The methodology integrates data from HMRC, ONS, ASHE, and the House of Commons Library, using gradient-based optimisation to calibrate over 11,000 targets covering income brackets, age bands, and employment patterns across all constituencies and local authorities.

The interactive dashboard allows users to explore local area data and compare impacts across regions, supporting analysis of income, consumption, wealth, taxes, benefits, and fiscal impacts at the constituency and local authority level. Applications include how Members of Parliament can understand how proposed reforms would affect their specific constituencies, and how local authorities can assess the implications of benefit changes for their communities. The map below illustrates the impact of removing the two-child limit across parliamentary constituencies, revealing geographical variation in how this policy reform would affect different regions of the UK. The technical challenges of producing geographically granular analysis whilst maintaining statistical validity demonstrate how this local dimension adds context to national policy debates. The framework represents an advance in UK policy analysis, enabling evidence-based discussions that account for regional variation in economic circumstances, demographic composition, and policy impacts.

NIESR's use of PolicyEngine in its living standards review

Speaker: Max Mosley

PolicyEngine was integrated into NIESR's UK Living Standards Review 2025. The review revealed findings about UK economic stagnation: the poorest households in Slovenia and Malta are now better off than the poorest in the UK, real income growth has been among the slowest in Europe, and weak productivity is costing UK workers thousands of pounds per year. Whilst real incomes grew consistently before the financial crisis, the stagnation afterwards has meant other countries have overtaken the UK standard of living. The UK now ranks as neither a high wage nor high welfare country, with welfare covering the cost of essentials in only two of the last fourteen years.

PolicyEngine enabled NIESR to analyse policy interventions. The platform was valuable for evaluating poverty reduction measures. The chart below illustrates policy choices by the impact on poverty and cost to the exchequer from the UK Living Standards Review 2025, comparing policy interventions including removing the two-child limit, increasing Universal Credit, and adjusting tax thresholds. The analysis reveals that removing the two-child limit would be cost-effective for reducing poverty, lifting households out of deprivation whilst allowing comparison of fiscal impacts across policy designs. PolicyEngine's accessibility and transparency improved the efficiency of NIESR's analysis and the communication of findings to policymakers and the public, providing an example of how open-source microsimulation tools complement economic research workflows at institutions.

Microsimulation of a carbon dividend

Speaker: Malcolm Torry

PolicyEngine's application to carbon tax and dividend proposals examines scenarios where carbon tax revenue funds unconditional payments to every individual. The analysis examines two scenarios: a £100 per tonne carbon tax generating £67.3 billion in revenue and funding a dividend of £19 per person per week, and a £200 per tonne tax generating £134.6 billion and funding a dividend of £37 per person per week. Carbon dividends address the challenge of ensuring carbon taxes don't reduce household disposable incomes, particularly for lower-income families who spend proportionally more on energy.

The left chart shows household income changes by decile for the £100 per tonne scenario, revealing that whilst income deciles 1-6 gain, deciles 8-10 experience net losses. The right chart shows the distribution of households by income change: approximately 71% of UK residents would benefit financially on a net basis, with 42% gaining more than 5% and 29% gaining less than 5%, while 28% would pay more in the carbon tax than they receive in the dividend. Lower income deciles show higher proportions of households gaining more than 5%, while higher income deciles are more likely to experience losses. The analysis shows poverty reduction of 0.3% at the £100/tonne rate and 0.5% at the £200/tonne rate. Geographic variation reveals that 265 constituencies would gain at £100/tonne, with urban areas such as Birmingham Ladywood, Bradford West, and Leeds South experiencing the largest gains as a share of net income.

VAT analysis with synthetic firm microdata

Speaker: Vahid Ahmadi

PolicyEngine's first application of firm-level microsimulation to UK tax policy analyses how VAT threshold changes between £70,000 and £120,000 would affect government revenues and VAT-registered businesses in 2026-27. With the current threshold at £90,000 and Chancellor Rachel Reeves reportedly considering further increases, this analysis addresses a gap by incorporating business-side tax impacts. The methodology constructs synthetic firm microdata through multi-objective optimisation, calibrated to match both ONS UK Business statistics and HMRC VAT statistics, capturing firms by industry, turnover bands, employee counts, and VAT liability.

The left chart shows the distribution of UK firms by annual turnover in 2024, revealing density drops above both the £90,000 VAT threshold and the £150,000 VAT Flat Rate Scheme limit, suggesting businesses constrain growth to avoid these thresholds. The right chart validates the model against HMRC's official projections for 2024-29, with PolicyEngine's estimates deviating an average of only 12.3% from HMRC. Findings for 2026-27: raising the threshold to £100,000 would remove approximately 48,000 firms and reduce revenue by £371 million, whilst a £70,000 threshold would add 109,000 firms and generate £642 million. Dynamic modelling can incorporate firm decisions and expectations of future turnover growth, capturing behavioural responses where businesses adjust operations based on threshold levels. Upcoming applications include sector-specific thresholds, phase-in mechanisms, and policies addressing cliff effects.

PolicyEngine US: Modelling federal, state, and local policies

Speaker: Max Ghenis

PolicyEngine's US operations expanded in 2024 to achieve coverage of American tax and benefit policy. A notable achievement was the launch of the household-by-household interactive for the One Big Beautiful Bill Act (OBBBA), which allowed users to explore the legislation's effects on over 40,000 representative households using PolicyEngine's Enhanced Current Population Survey data. The interactive's "Get Random Household" function reveals how the same policy affects different families across America's diverse population, with each household weighted by population representation to ensure coverage. This capability showcases PolicyEngine's ability to deliver real-time analysis of legislation in ways that make policy impacts tangible.

PolicyEngine US's scope, achieved in 2024, includes federal tax and benefit programs including SNAP, TANF, childcare subsidies, and housing assistance; income tax models for all 50 states and DC; and tools like the SALT-AMT calculator and GiveCalc for charitable giving analysis. The Enhanced Current Population Survey integrates IRS data and Congressional Budget Office forecasts using machine learning to enable tax-benefit microsimulation. PolicyEngine has analysed Congressional proposals including the American Family Act and Tax Relief for Workers Act, partnerships with organisations like the Niskanen Center for Child Tax Credit research, and state-level work in Montana, Utah, New York, and Louisiana.

This expansion has been supported by grants from foundations and agencies. The National Science Foundation awarded PolicyEngine $300,000 through its Pathways to Enable Open-Source Ecosystems (POSE) program to strengthen organisational infrastructure and community engagement around economic policy analysis tools. Arnold Ventures has provided multiple awards supporting state and congressional district policy breakdowns, calculators for SALT and AMT policies, and labour supply response modelling. MyFriendBen contracted PolicyEngine to power its benefit eligibility screener with API support, while NEO Philanthropy provided general operating support through its Economic Mobility & Opportunity Fund. These partnerships demonstrate recognition of PolicyEngine's role in democratising policy analysis across the United States.

Panel discussion: The future of evidence-based policymaking

Panelists: Max Ghenis, Arun Advani, Anvar Sarygulov

This discussion explores the landscape of evidence-based policymaking and the directions for the field. Technological advances (including artificial intelligence, data infrastructure, and open-source collaboration) are changing the speed, scope, and accessibility of policy analysis. PolicyEngine's roadmap for capabilities includes incorporating macroeconomic impacts to transform static microsimulation into economic forecasting, modelling labour supply to capture saving, education, and family structure decisions, and expanding international reach with PolicyEngine-Canada and partnerships with think tanks and academic institutions worldwide.

The conversation explores questions about democratising policy analysis while maintaining analytical rigour. User experience improvements (sign-in functionality, mobile optimisation, and social collaboration features) broaden who can engage with policy tools. AI integration, including GPT-4-powered explanations and planned API access, makes tax and benefit calculations understandable for non-experts while preserving technical precision for researchers. The changing relationship between academic research, government analysis, and public engagement presents opportunities for public discourse and challenges of managing uncertainty and maintaining trust in computational models. The skills and institutional changes needed to realise the potential of policy modelling remain important for improving societal outcomes and strengthening democratic decision-making.

Looking ahead: AI-powered policy analysis

Speakers: Max Ghenis, Nikhil Woodruff, Vahid Ahmadi

A multi-agent AI research workflow has been developed and tested on policy analysis tasks similar to the UK carbon dividend study. Using Claude Code's multi-agent system, PolicyEngine has configured agents that automate different parts of the policy analysis pipeline: a data fetching agent that accesses PolicyEngine microsimulation data from GitHub repositories and documentation, a script writer that generates Python code using the policyengine-uk package, and a report generator that produces formatted research output with Plotly visualisations. Claude Code automatically routes tasks to appropriate agents based on natural language requests, enabling real-time policy analysis without manual co-ordination between components.

Testing revealed successes and limitations of this approach. Multi-agent workflows excel at distributional analyses (calculating poverty rates, Gini coefficients, and decile-level impacts), saving time on tasks while maintaining PolicyEngine's house style. However, challenges emerged with benefit programme interactions, the need for prompting rather than instructions, and the importance of reviewer agents for code critique. The forthcoming policyengine-claude plugin packages these insights for other researchers using Claude Code with PolicyEngine, incorporating Anthropic's Skills ecosystem for common policy analyses and direct database access through the Model Context Protocol. The division of labour: AI handles policy updates with constant analysis structures, while human researchers focus on questions requiring policy expertise, methodological choices, and political context, ensuring that AI serves to augment rather than replace judgment in evidence-based policymaking.
